Case Study · 03

Sofatica — Designed for Real Life.

Reimagining the e-commerce experience for a premium modular sofa brand built around a single uncomfortable truth: beautiful furniture has to survive kids, pets, and Tuesday-night spaghetti.

Role

UI/UX Designer

Year

2025

Tools

Figma, Maze, Webflow

Client

Sofatica

Visit Sofatica → View Prototype →

01 · The Challenge

What problem we solved.

Sofatica's flagship product — a $1,399–$7,997 modular sofa with machine-washable covers and a 100% goose-down fill — sat between two audiences that didn't trust each other. To luxury-furniture buyers, "machine-washable" sounded cheap. To young parents shopping IKEA-tier, the price felt unjustifiable.

The existing site leaned into the luxury aesthetic — large hero photography, minimal copy, generous white space — but the messaging didn't earn it. Conversion was [XX]% below benchmark for the price tier. Cart abandonment was concentrated on the configurator page, where users had to make four sequential decisions (size, color, version, modules) before seeing a final price.

The brief: build a shopping experience that wins both audiences without picking a side. Convince luxury buyers the engineering is real. Convince young parents the price is worth it. And get them through the configurator without losing them halfway.

I love how this sofa looks. I just need to know it'll survive my three-year-old before I spend four grand. — Sofatica customer, post-interview

02 · Process

How we got there.

We ran nine 60-minute interviews split across two profiles: established homeowners (35–55, dual-income, design-conscious) and young parents (28–38, first home, time-constrained). Two patterns came back, in different words but identical meaning.

Both groups wanted proof of durability before they cared about aesthetics. Both wanted to compare directly to RH and West Elm — the brands they'd already shortlisted — without leaving the site or hunting through forums.

We restructured the page priorities accordingly. Hero photography stayed beautiful, but the second viewport became proof: a 30-second video of cover removal, the goose-down + foam construction shown in cross-section, the ModuleSecure™ clip-lock demonstrated in real time. The comparison table — Sofatica vs. RH vs. fast-furniture — moved from the FAQ to mid-page.

The configurator was the hardest problem. Four sequential decisions with no clear ordering was killing conversion. We rebuilt it as a 3D room planner with progressive disclosure: start with seater count, see live pricing, then refine modules. By the third interaction the user has already mentally bought a sofa.

03 · The Solution

The final design.

The final design opens with one strong photograph and a single line of copy — "Designed for real life" — that lets the brand voice land before the value proposition. Within one scroll, you've seen the cover removed, the construction explained, and the price compared against two competitors.

The configurator is now a single page with three states: Explore (browse configurations), Customize (modify in 3D), Confirm (review + checkout). The 3D viewer responds to thumb gestures on mobile and click-drag on desktop. Switching colors swaps the material map without a full page reload — a small detail that turned out to drive a measurable lift in time-on-page.

The Reviews page — usually a graveyard for star ratings — became a curated section organized by buyer concern: "pets," "kids," "small spaces," "allergies." Each filter surfaces both photos and longer-form reviews, with verified-buyer badges and date stamps that make the content feel investigative rather than promotional.

The Why Sofatica page consolidates every proof point into a single 5-minute read: materials, warranty terms, return policy, comparison breakdown, sustainability documentation. It exists for the buyer who needs to feel they've done their due diligence — and once they have, the decision is easy.

Numbers aside, the qualitative shift was bigger. Customer-support inquiries that used to start with "is this actually machine-washable?" largely disappeared — the home-page video answered it before the question was typed. Sales-team time on first-touch calls dropped because prospects were arriving informed.

What I learned working on Sofatica: in categories where buyers carry identity tension — "am I a luxury buyer or a practical buyer?" — the design doesn't have to resolve it. It just has to make both stories feel coherent on the same page. Beautiful and practical aren't opposites. But the site has to prove that on every viewport, not just claim it once.
