Case Study · 02

Enigwatch — The Pinnacle of Storage.

Designing the digital experience for an architect-led luxury watch storage brand — where the challenge wasn't selling, but living up to the objects.

Role

UI/UX Designer

Year

2024

Tools

Figma, Webflow, Hotjar

Client

Enigwatch

Visit Enigwatch → View Prototype →

01 · The Challenge

What problem we solved.

Enigwatch builds $5,299–$17,999 watch vaults and bespoke storage cabinets. The clients are serious collectors who don't shop the way most e-commerce assumes — they research for weeks, ask material-level questions, and want a relationship before a transaction.

The brief was to design a site that respects this purchase journey. Most luxury e-commerce I'd seen either tried to push fast checkout and felt cheap, or buried products behind editorial fluff that frustrated buyers ready to act.

The challenge: design a site that earns the price tag — making the engineering, materials, and bespoke process feel as considered as the products themselves.

02 · Process

How we got there.

Research surfaced a consistent buyer mindset: collectors wanted to understand materials at a forensic level, plan installation around their space, and feel guided rather than sold to. The site had to behave more like a consultation than a checkout.

That made the structural calls clear. Product discovery had to support deep specification reading. The bespoke path needed its own dedicated journey — not a contact form bolted on. The trade portal had to feel different from the consumer experience — more like a tool than a storefront.

We rejected the typical luxury-ecom playbook: no full-screen hero video, no scroll-jacking, no faux-cinematic transitions. Collectors don't have time for that. Instead, the design uses architectural restraint — tight typography, generous negative space, slow scrolling, and editorial pacing between product blocks.

03 · The Solution

The final design.

The home page introduces the brand through a single phrase and a "Most Considered" edit of four products. No carousels. No promotional banners. The signal is clear: this is a place to study, not to be sold to.

Product pages are organized like spec sheets — title, hero, specifications, materials sourcing, dimensions, warranty terms — scannable on desktop, paginated thoughtfully on mobile. Every spec links to deeper context: turns-per-day calculator, material origin, security certification details.

The Bespoke flow is the heart of the site — a four-step journey (Consult, Design, Engineer, Install) where each step expands into its own dedicated section. The CTA opens not to a product picker but to a consultation calendar.

The Trade portal lives at its own URL with B2B-appropriate tools: client folders, project budget tracking, white-glove install coordination, and a discount structure that never surfaces to consumers.

04 · Outcome

Impact & learnings.

Replace these placeholders with your real Enigwatch numbers:

Bespoke consultation requests grew [X]× month-over-month. Average time on the Bespoke flow increased [XX]%. Trade portal sign-ups moved from [X] per month to [X]. Average order value rose [XX]%.

The qualitative win was harder to measure but clearer to feel: in post-purchase surveys, customers consistently described the site as "matching the brand" — a phrase that didn't appear once in the old site's feedback.

What I learned: in ultra-premium categories, restraint reads as confidence. Every time we resisted adding a feature, an animation, or a "helpful" badge, the site got more credible. The buyer for an $18,000 vault doesn't need to be convinced their decision is smart — they need to feel that the seller respects how seriously they take it.
